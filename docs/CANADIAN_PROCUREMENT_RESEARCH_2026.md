## Positioning recommendations for ContinuityOS

1. **Lead with operational continuity, not generic “AI for national security.”**  
   Stronger language: *“A secure coordination and evidence layer for continuity, incident response, exercises, recovery, and cross-organization readiness.”*

2. **Target the seams between existing systems.**  
   The procurement evidence points to GIS, AIS, radar, SIEM, sensors, infrastructure, and engineering systems already being bought. ContinuityOS should complement these systems by coordinating:
   - incidents and decisions;
   - dependencies and critical services;
   - tasking and accountable owners;
   - exercises and after-action remediation;
   - continuity plans and recovery evidence;
   - contractor / operator / government handoffs.

3. **Prioritize realistic buyers and channels.**
   - Transport Canada / CCG / DND operational and infrastructure teams;
   - Public Safety Canada and emergency-management partners;
   - territorial, municipal, port, airport, and Indigenous infrastructure operators;
   - defence primes, shipyards, EPC providers, and systems integrators;
   - research / pilot consortia under cyber or dual-use innovation programs.

4. **Build procurement readiness into the product posture.**
   - Canadian hosting / data-residency options where appropriate;
   - clear tenant isolation and access controls;
   - audit logs and exportable evidence;
   - security-control mapping;
   - incident-response and business-continuity documentation;
   - CPCSC readiness for defence-supply-chain participation;
   - explicit boundaries between unclassified, protected, controlled, and classified use.

## Claims ContinuityOS must **not** make

- **No Government of Canada endorsement, partnership, approval, or deployment** unless a signed and public agreement proves it.
- **No claim of being “approved by CSE,” “CSE-compatible,” or compliant with CPCSC** without formal evidence and scope-specific assessment.
- **No claim that ContinuityOS is a SIEM, cyber-defence sensor, AIS provider, radar system, classified command-and-control system, or combat-system integrator** unless those capabilities genuinely exist and are independently validated.
- **No claim that it protects Canadian critical infrastructure nationally** based solely on policy priorities, CSE activity, or funding announcements.
- **No claim of a contract, pilot, RFP invitation, or buyer interest** from the Public Safety disaster-recovery tender; the source confirms a tender, not a ContinuityOS award.
- **No claim that Budget 2025 allocations are available grants or committed ContinuityOS procurement.** They are policy and spending authorizations, not purchase orders.
- **No claim that Arctic deployments or infrastructure projects require ContinuityOS.** Use “potential use case,” “adjacent need,” or “integration opportunity.”
- **No claim that “Canadian-made,” “sovereign,” “secure,” “zero trust,” “Five Eyes interoperable,” or “classified-ready” applies without a documented architecture, contract scope, and assessment.
- **No implication that non-competitive or directed government awards indicate a market opening for ContinuityOS.** Several cited awards were capability-specific, incumbent-dependent, or based on exclusive rights.
- **No suggestion that Nanisivik’s transition reflects an Arctic capability failure.** DND explicitly states the RCN’s Arctic operating capability is not compromised and is shifting investment toward other infrastructure and support models.
