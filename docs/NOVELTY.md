# Novelty and Defensibility

## Bounded claim

Individual components are not novel by themselves: digital twins, supply-chain graphs, maritime awareness, risk scoring, policy engines, optimization, event sourcing, and signed audit logs all have prior art.

The potentially differentiating engineering combination is:

1. **Source-qualified operational assertions** that prevent public context data from masquerading as live cyber or capacity truth.
2. **Cyber-to-physical dependency propagation** focused on continuity of critical trade corridors.
3. **Functional closure assessment** based on availability, integrity, commercial confidence, logistics support, and missing-data uncertainty—not physical closure alone.
4. **Continuity compilation** from an explicit service objective into a feasible, costed, dependency-aware mitigation set.
5. **Evidence-bearing decisions** that preserve source snapshots, constraints, alternatives, solver method, approval, and outcome.
6. **Offline reproducibility** designed for intermittent and sovereign environments.

This is a credible product differentiation thesis. It is not, by itself, proof of legal patent novelty or non-obviousness.

## Protectable layers

- domain ontology for cyber-physical trade corridor dependencies;
- source assertion policy packs and validated transforms;
- functional closure calibration models;
- substitution feasibility and recovery-lag models;
- customer-specific integration adapters;
- decision-outcome dataset;
- accredited sovereign deployment patterns;
- insurer and operator workflow integration.

## Weak or non-defensible layers

- generic map dashboard;
- public AIS aggregation;
- generic geopolitical alerts;
- simple weighted risk score;
- use of Terraform, Kubernetes, STAC, OGC APIs, or a graph database;
- a generic LLM summarizer;
- public sea-ice or orbital data.

## Recommended IP work

Before filing patents or representing the product as proprietary:

- commission a professional prior-art search;
- document the exact claims and technical mechanisms;
- preserve dated architecture records and test evidence;
- separate trade secrets from patent disclosures;
- avoid claiming ownership over public data or standards;
- validate freedom to operate for third-party libraries and datasets.
